The QR Code on the jeans does not work anymore.
It has expired.


Here's what happened.


Original Work

At the beginning of last semester, I made a project called "Don’t Ask Me Anything Jeans", a pair of jeans with a QR code that leads to a website. On it is a list of answers to all the questions I have been asked ever since I came to the US. It was a lighthearted work of cynicism at the never-ending icebreakers at the start of every semester.


What Happened

I simply went to the website that popped up at the top of the search result for ‘best QR code generator’. Their advertisement slogan “FREE QR Code” seemed like a good deal. Two weeks later, they sent me an email to accounce that my QR code was going to expire unless I upgrade to a paid subscription plan. The cheapest was $32.23 per month, totalling $96.69 for 3 months. Way too much money for a single QR code.

Only then I searched for some reviews for this company and found a handful of furious people who had to throw away their business cards and already printed QR signs. They called this company an "absolute scam". I should have looked up the review before I used their website. I should have read their terms and conditions. I should have been more prudent and careful, but it was too late. I should have, I should have.


What I did

Rather than devoting another pair of jeans to spray-paint on a new QR code, I decided to gear this project toward something else; an anecdote about a downside of consumerism. This is an example of what happens when people are not as vigilant in the face of aggressive marketing practices.

Takeaways

The QR code on these jeans does not lead to my project page anymore.
But, at least, I can hope that you may learn from my failure to make a well-informed financial decision.
All in all, stay awake, fellow consumers! Know what you are agreeing to, and know what you are buying!
